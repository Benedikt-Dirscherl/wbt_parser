Getting Started With Horizontal and Vertical System Integration

System integration is one of the core pillars when it comes to industry 4.0 and the smart factory. Let's watch the video, to find out the reason behind this.

We all live in a fast-changing, interconnected and data-driven world. The internet makes all kinds of information constantly available and through mobile data and smartphones, everybody has access to them at any point in time and from anywhere in the world. People living thousands of miles apart from each other can communicate and keep each other up-to-date in real-time via messaging services and social networks.
In recent years though, not only people, but more and more devices have been connected to the internet as well. Today, we can check if we really switched off the oven at home, when we’re already in the subway on the way to work. And the best thing about this is: We can do this very easily with our finger and with only one device – our smartphone. Our smartphone with all its apps, webservices, etc. enables us to manage our entire personal life with one swipe. 
But it was not always that easy. Back in the early days of smartphones, certain apps often only worked with the operating systems of certain smartphone manufacturers. But what has changed since then? What enabled the kind of simplicity we’re experiencing today? The concept behind this development is called integration. Integration allows us to access data from all kinds of sources at a single access point. Furthermore, it allows us to control the devices remotely and to make decisions based on real-time information. If, for example, you’re unsure about whether to take your dog for a long walk in the morning or in the afternoon, you can easily check the weather via your smartphone and make a decision based on the forecast. 
So far so good. At least for our private lives. But what does this look like in the company we’re working for? First of all, there definitely have been changes in the environment of companies as well. Especially the market requirements have been growing significantly. Due to resource shortage and climate change, an efficient use of resources has become more important than ever before. Product life cycles as well as innovation cycles have been shortened extremely. Also, our customer’s demands regarding the functionality and variety of our products have been rising substantially. In order to cope with these changed conditions, we must design our production more flexibly. To do so, we need to collect and process as much data as possible and extract information from which we can gain knowledge that helps us to make decisions and improve flexibility. Thus, data becomes the future fuel for our company’s engine.
There are already plenty of data sources in traditional companies today. Unfortunately, in most cases, this data is stored in silos with limited access for selected employees. You can think of these data silos as databases stored locally in the network of a specific department. It is neither being put into relation with data from other departments, nor is there a central point of access. This poses a major problem. If, for example a top manager needs exact information about the hourly output of a certain production site, he needs to call somebody on site, who can then manually transfer the requested data via mail. This happens in the form of CSV tables or excel files and the information must then be processed manually – again. One reason for this is that different systems speak different languages and they often can’t talk or be connected to each other. As a result of different system and information structures, this leads to typical problems of insufficient interfaces. Because the systems don’t work together perfectly, the collection and processing of data often means a lot of manual work and becomes a time-consuming task. Idle and lead times are long, redundant data processing activities occur regularly, misunderstandings drive up costs of bad quality, and so on and so on. 
As we can see, the data we already have is often not utilized efficiently and fails to deliver sufficient benefit to our company. If we want to change that, we need to integrate. Just like with our smartphones in our personal lives, we need the possibility to access, process and share data and information at one single access point. An access point that can be reached by anybody from anywhere at any time. The process behind creating this access point is called integration. Only then we can make profound decisions based on high quality information and thus enable efficiency, transparency and flexibility. The main problem here is that this transformation process is very dependent on the existing systems. They are often very inflexible and rigid. Also, they lack standardized interfaces. Thus, we need to eliminate hierarchical structures and establish a more flexible, dynamic, and network-like system landscape, which is exactly what integration is all about.

What Kinds of Different Systems Exist in Companies? – An Overview

As described in the video, industry 4.0 aims at connecting all kinds of data sources with each other to improve efficiency. Different systems across the company can be sources for such data and business activities can be supported by many different kinds of software and systems. So, let’s have a look at the existing system landscape in traditional companies.

Management Level

Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) systems are responsible for supporting companies in the planning of all of their resources. That includes for example the rough planning of production and the order management. The ERP connects databases from different functional areas with each other and therefore provides a hub for all data. For example, imagine the ERP of an automobile manufacturer. They usually produce cars in batches, which means that as soon as there is a certain number of orders the production of a batch starts. Therefore, the sales team takes orders and enters them into the ERP system. The data is then automatically checked against existing inventory levels and transferred to the purchasing department in order to buy materials if necessary and to the HR department in order to plan the deployment of individual employees. ERP systems thus help to plan all resources of an enterprise at a strategic level and provide a data hub for the entire company. Due to the long-term orientation these plans have a relatively low level of detail.

Planning Level

In today’s fast-changing environment, events that influence the optimal allocation of resources can happen basically every minute. Imagine a situation in which an automobile manufacturer sends a change request to his supplier in which he demands to change the ordered seat color from blue to black one day before the batch is produced. In order to react to such events, we need to design our production processes as well as our planning systems in a flexible way. Manufacturing Executions Systems (MES) act as a supplement to ERP systems and help to control production processes with a higher level of detail on a more tactical basis. In our example above the change request by the customer can be entered into the MES, which then adapts the daily production plan in order to fulfil the customer’s demand. Furthermore, MES help to continuously improve production process efficiency through the collection and processing of data of a complete production site. Also, they transparently reflect all information and material flows.

Supervisory Level

Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA) systems are responsible for supervising (and to some extent controlling) technical processes on a single-machine level, as opposed to the entire production site (like on the planning level). SCADA systems collect and process data from the lower levels of the automation pyramid. Think of an oil-pipeline. Sensors that measure the throughput of the pipeline transfer the data in real-time. The SCADA system supervises this data and draws information from it. For example, it realizes that the pipeline leaks at some point if the oil input is higher than the output. The system would transfer the information to the human supervisor in form of an alert sound in combination with a pop-up window on the screen of the machine. The human supervisor can then decide whether the oil flow has to be stopped to fix the leak or not.

Control Level

The devices that are used on the control level are called Programmable Logic Controllers (PLCs). You can think of PLCs as small industrial computers with modular components designed to automate customized control processes. But to give you a practical example, you can think of your dishwasher. The dishwasher has inputs (start button and a water sensor), outputs (water valves) and a PLC. When you push the start button the PLC detects the input and executes the programmed steps. In this case it opens the fill valve and the dishwasher begins filling with water. When the water sensor gives the PLC the input that the water level is high enough, the PLC closes the fill valve and the water stops flowing. This logic is applicable to many use cases in factories and industrial plants to control motors, pumps, lights, fans, circuit-breakers and other machinery.

Field Level

The field level basically provides the control level with the necessary inputs and outputs. Devices on the field level can be divided into two groups. On the one hand we have sensors like the water sensor in the dishwasher example above. Sensors provide the inputs for the PLC. On the other hand, there are actuators. In our dish washer example, the water valve opens and closes depending on the input data from the sensor and is thus an actuator. Actuators execute the output they get from the PLC. Inputs and outputs are often abbreviated with the term “I/O”. On the shop floor sensors and actuators are usually connected with the PLCs through “I/O-Link devices”. They transfer the data between the PLC and the hardware.

What Is the Difference Between Horizontal and Vertical Integration?

We already learned that horizontal and vertical integration create connections between all kinds of data sources and systems. But what exactly does integration mean and what is the difference between vertical and horizontal integration?

Click on the plus icons to get more information!

Vertical Integration

Integration of systems from the top management level down to the field level

Companies can be divided into different functional areas like sourcing, production, and sales. Every functional area consists of several hierarchical layers. Since data can be collected wherever processes take place, people work and sensors transmit signals, data sources can be found in every functional area and at every hierarchical level. As explained above, in production for example, we find the ERP, MES and SCADA systems, PLCs, sensors and actuators from the management level all the way down to the field level.

More Information

In order to break down data silos which exist on the separate hierarchical levels and to create relations between the different data sources, vertical integration connects all systems of one functional area. 

Think of a sales person entering customer orders into the ERP system, which had already generated a rough production plan based on forecasts. But the exact production program for the day is based on actual orders and generated by the MES. Therefore, we need a reliable communication between the MES and the ERP. This connection and the exchange of information between the management and the planning level is called vertical integration.

Horizontal Integration 

Integration of systems across functional areas as well as across company borders

To achieve increased flexibility and production speed, industry 4.0 enforces order-driven as opposed to forecast-driven production processes. Order-driven production ideally means that every individual product is produced for one specific customer order. Due to this development data must not only flow freely between hierarchical layers, but also along the entire value chain. Within one company, horizontal integration thus describes the interconnection of data sources across different functional areas such as e.g. production and sales. 

More Information

But a value chain usually does not end at the borders of one company. In the age of industry 4.0, close collaboration with other players in the industry becomes more and more important. Therefore, horizontal integration also includes the interconnection of a company’s data with data from suppliers, customers and partners. For example, a supplier can provide an electronic catalogue and the customer can then order via an integrated platform. Utilizing the available data bases, the sales order can then trigger for example the payment on the buyer side and ordering of new materials on the supplier side.

Which Requirements Does Our System Landscape Need to Fulfill?

Due to the increase of global competition, the shortening of product lifecycles, the changes in consumer behavior and the creation of networks and ecosystems, companies are required to design their processes more flexible, efficient and transparent. Vertical and horizontal integration play a major role in the fulfillment of this task. But to achieve the goal of a fully integrated system landscape, there are several requirements. So, let’s have a look at what is necessary to accomplish full integration.

Click on the arrows to get more information!

Data Transfer and Storage

As described above traditional companies have several functional departments and operate on diverse hierarchical levels. In order to achieve a higher degree of flexibility in a globalized world, all data must be fully available to anybody in the company, at any point in time, and from any geographical place. Making the data available requires that the data can easily be transferred between all departments and hierarchical levels. Additionally, to ensure a high level of data accessibility, the data should be stored centrally.

Connectivity and Communication

We already know that there are many different systems and data sources within a single company. To achieve full data availability and accessibility, the different systems and machines must be able to communicate – respectively “speak” – to each other. In the status quo it is often the case that if an employee from the production department needs information about the planned sales for the next three months, he needs to call somebody who has access to that specific data to manually transfer the information via mail. In order to change this, we need to enable different systems to connect and communicate with each other.

Stability and Scalability

Already today we have hundreds or thousands of data sources in the form of sensors on the shop floor of a company. The amounts of data collected and processed have increased exponentially within the past years and this development will most likely continue. Also, collaboration and integration with suppliers, partners and customers will further increase. These trends result in a demand for scalable and stable platforms that grow adequately with the number of data sources and points of connection. Let us say we want to integrate a new customer in our system for vendor managed inventory. To ensure a fast and effortless integration, we need a scalable and stable platform on which we can perform the integration.

Flexibility and Adaptability

We already learned, that flexibility and adaptability of systems is crucial in order to integrate successfully and to create a free flow of data. As described in the introductory video one problem are rigid hierarchical structures of existing systems. They make it hard to flexibly adapt the system landscape to the dynamic environment and its demands. Therefore, it is crucial to move away from a static hierarchy architecture towards a flexible and dynamic network architecture. That enables a quick adaptation of products and services without having to perform time-consuming and expensive software updates. For example, that means having modular software tools which we can configure exactly the way we need it.

Which Challenges Are We Facing While Integrating Our System Landscape?

We now know what the requirements are for ensuring a fully integrated system landscape. First we need continuous and reliable data accessibility and availability but also connectivity and communication between systems across functional areas / company borders and hierarchical levels. Additionally we also need scalable and stable platforms, as well as a flexible and adaptable system landscape. But why do we not already fulfill these requirements? Which challenges are we facing in the horizontal and vertical integration?

Interfaces

In order to transfer data between people, sensors, machines and all the different systems we need interfaces. Interfaces are responsible for the communication and can be seen as points of data transfer. A human-machine interface for example could be the screen of an industrial oven over which the employee controls the temperature. But machines and software from different vendors use different interfaces or sometimes do not even have any – you can think of razor blades from one brand that are not compatible with the shavers of another brand. Imagine a situation where a sales force employee needs information from the sourcing department. But the sales force uses a standard software and the sourcing department exclusively uses a special software, developed by the company itself. Without a common interface they cannot exchange necessary information. Thus, it is necessary to create interfaces that are horizontally and vertically compatible, which means that they can connect systems across functional areas and company borders as well as across hierarchical layers.

Communication Protocols

In order to exchange data between different systems, rules and information on the communication is necessary. In the field of information technology, communication protocols stipulate these rules. One of the most popular examples is the Internet Protocol (IP). You can think of a communication protocol as a set of rules that determines the grammar and the vocabulary of a language. When machines need to talk to each other, we face the same challenges as when people need to talk to each other. They need to speak the same language and both parties need a common understanding of the rules of the used language. Thus, we need standardized communication protocols to enable a flawless and reliable communication and connectivity between different systems.

Legacy Systems

Another challenge we have to deal with are the restrictions of the automation pyramid in our legacy systems of traditional Industry 3.0. As stated above we need to create compatible interfaces as well as standardized communication protocols, since these are often non-existent. Another challenge is the low flexibility of many legacy systems, which is due to software and system functions being mostly predefined and difficult to update. A usually very static model and strict hierarchies further impair their flexibility. That means that there is a strict allocation of tasks and that systems are only able to recognize and perform predefined tasks and processes. These restrictions make it hard to transform legacy systems into more integrated solutions.

Willingness to Change and Trust

Apart from the purely technical challenges regarding interfaces, communication protocols and legacy systems, we are also facing several challenges regarding organizational and cultural aspects. Especially in collaborations with suppliers, customers or competitors a certain degree of trust is crucial for a successful partnership. But as important as trusting the partner are clear objectives for the partnership, a continuous exchange of information and knowledge, and a high level of data control and security. Also, every employee should be aware of potential cultural differences within and across company borders. Furthermore, every company needs to protect itself from becoming dependent on another company.

Lessons Learned

Let’s briefly summarize the key learnings from this chapter!

  • There are several vertically organized hierarchical levels within a traditional company. Reaching from top of the automation pyramid down to the bottom. Each level deploys different software and systems which can be connected through vertical integration.
  • Collaboration in Industry 4.0 does not only take part across functional areas of one company, but across the entire value chain, thus including customers, partners and suppliers. They can all be connected through horizontal integration.
  • Main requirements for a fully integrated system landscape are sufficient data availability and accessibility, connectivity and communication between the different systems, scalability of the used platforms and flexible and adaptable systems.
  • Creating compatible system interfaces and consistent communication protocols, transforming legacy systems as well as trust in organizations and alliances are major challenges for horizontal and vertical integration.
  • Innovative technologies and concepts such as cloud computing, OPC-UA, retrofitting and change and alliance management can help us to overcome the challenges of integration.
